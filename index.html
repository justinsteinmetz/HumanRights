Part 2 — Concept

Three Distinctions

⏱ 5 minutes

Before we discuss which rights matter, we need to agree on what a right actually is.

A right is not the same as…

A need — something your body or mind requires to survive. You need food. But having a need doesn't automatically mean anyone is obliged to provide it.

A privilege — something you have because of where or who you were born. A privilege can be taken away. It depends on luck or status.

A right — a claim that every person has simply because they are human. Not earned. Not given by a government. Not dependent on nationality.

The hard question

If rights belong to everyone, equally, by birth — then who is responsible for protecting them?

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) was written after the Holocaust and World War II. It tried to answer that question. But it was also written mostly by Western nations — countries that were, at that same moment, running empires in Africa and Asia.

Part 3 — Language

How to Argue This in English

⏱ 2 minutes

You'll need these phrases in the discussion. Read them once, then close the tab and try to use at least two.

Claiming a position

I would argue that…gives your opinion formal weight
The key issue here is…redirects to what matters
That's more of a privilege than a right because…uses the concept
You could say it's a need, but…concedes, then qualifies

Challenging politely

I'm not sure that's quite right…soft disagreement
That depends on who you're asking.introduces power / perspective
But who decides that?the structural question
What about someone who doesn't have access to that?shifts to the margins

Stretch phrases

The problem with that argument is that it assumes…identifies a hidden assumption
That's universally claimed but not universally enforced.theory vs. practice

Note on register: In a real discussion or MUN context, you avoid "I think" — you argue. These phrases help you sound more formal without sounding robotic.

Part 4 — Discussion

Structured Discussion

⏱ 7 minutes — pairs or threes, then brief whole class

5:00 Pair discussion

Choose one scenario to discuss. Argue your position using the phrases from Part 3.

A
Scenario A — Accessible

A government says: "You have the right to vote — but only if you pass a literacy test."
Is this still a right? Why / why not?

B
Scenario B — Core

Some countries say access to the internet is a basic right. Others say it's a luxury. Who gets to make that decision — and does it matter who is making it?

C
Scenario C — Stretch

The nations that wrote the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948 were also controlling colonies at that time. Does that affect whether we should trust the document? Or do rights belong to everyone regardless of who wrote them down?

Whole-class pull (2 mins)

Ask each group: What was the hardest thing to agree on, and why?

Don't resolve it. Just surface the disagreement.

Part 5 — Close

One Question Out

⏱ 2 minutes

Exit question — no right answer required

If rights are universal — belonging to every human, everywhere — but most of the world lacks the power to enforce them, what does that tell us about where power actually sits?

Students write one sentence in response — or say it aloud as they leave.

Lesson summary

  • Rights ≠ needs ≠ privileges — but the lines are contested
  • Universal claims were made by non-universal voices
  • The question who decides? is always a power question
